Because natural gas is expensive to store, its futures curve is rarely a straight line. It looks like a rollercoaster:

The Winter Premium (Backwardation): During peak winter (January/February), immediate demand is so high that the near-term contracts trade at a massive premium to the spring contracts. When the near-term price is higher than the future price, the curve is in Backwardation.

The Summer Build (Contango): During the spring "shoulder season," there is excess gas, so it gets injected into storage. The market prices future winter contracts higher than current spring contracts to account for the "cost of carry" (storage costs). This upward slope is Contango.

While historical seasonality shows a drop in demand, we analyzed how this was priced into the futures term structure. Looking at the curve, the transition from the March winter contract to the April spring contract features a steep structural drop. >
The market here is in backwardation—pricing March gas much higher than April gas due to the sudden evaporation of heating demand before the spring shoulder season begins. By buying Puts, we were effectively monetizing this exact transition in the curve, profiting as the market shifted out of winter backwardation and into spring contango.
